Why a Driveway Change Order Template Matters

Even the best-planned driveway project can shift. You might decide to widen the apron, upgrade to stamped concrete, or move a drain line after the first pour. Without a clear paper trail, those “small” tweaks snowball into surprise invoices, delays, and strained contractor relationships. A driveway change order template is the simple, one-page form that keeps everyone honest.

Think of it as a mini-contract that records:

  • Exactly what is changing
  • How much it costs
  • How the schedule is affected
  • Who authorized the work

Below, we’ll walk you through when to use one, what belongs on the form, and how to handle pricing, permits, and disputes—so your project stays on budget and on time.

When You Need a Driveway Change Order

Not every tweak warrants a formal change order. Use the template whenever the change affects price, timeline, or scope.

Common Triggers

  • Material upgrade: Switching from standard asphalt to heated driveway pavers.
  • Size change: Adding 100 sq ft to park a second boat.
  • Site conditions: Hitting unexpected clay that requires geo-grid reinforcement.
  • Code update: City inspector requires an extra expansion joint.
  • Customer request: Adding a decorative stamped border after forms are already set.

When You Can Skip It

If the contractor simply swaps one brand of rebar for an equal-cost equivalent, a quick email confirmation is enough. If dollars or days change, pull out the template.

Essential Parts of a Driveway Change Order Template

Keep the form to one page; busy crews won’t fill out novels. Create a simple table in Word or Google Docs, or download our free fill-in-the-blank version at the end of this article.

1. Header

  • Project address
  • Original contract date & number
  • Change order number (start at 001, increment for each change)
  • Date the change order is written

2. Description of Change

Be laser-specific. Instead of “add decorative edge,” write “Install 6” wide stamped concrete border in Ashlar slate pattern, 4” thick, color ‘Smoke,’ around entire 20’ × 24’ driveway perimeter.”

3. Cost Breakdown

  • Labor (extra hours × rate)
  • Materials (line-item each)
  • Equipment rental (e.g., extra saw cuts)
  • Subtotal, tax, grand total

4. Schedule Impact

“Adds 1 working day to schedule. New substantial completion date: July 14, 2025.”

5. Payment Terms

Due on signing? Added to final invoice? 50 % upfront for special-order materials? Spell it out.

6. Signatures

Homeowner, contractor, and (if applicable) lender or HOA rep. Digital signatures via DocuSign are legally binding in most states.

How to Create Your Own Driveway Change Order Template

Step 1: Pick a Format

Excel and Google Sheets auto-calculate totals. PDFs look professional on mobile. Choose the tool you’ll actually edit in the truck or kitchen.

Step 2: Build a Master Template

Save a blank version titled “Driveway-Change-Order-Master.” Lock header fields so you can’t accidentally delete them.

Step 3: Add Dropdowns for Speed

For material choices, create data-validation lists (e.g., “concrete, asphalt, pavers, gravel”). It prevents typos and speeds up field entry.

Step 4: Brand It

Insert your contractor logo and license number. Homeowners feel more confident signing a polished form.

Step 5: Test It

Run a fake change (widen drive 2 ft) and confirm math cells add correctly. Email it to yourself and open on phone to check readability.

How to Price Changes Fairly

Get 2–3 Mini-Quotes

For changes over $1 k, ask subs to email a quick quote. Even if you stick with the original crew, you’ll know the market rate.

Use Unit Prices from the Original Bid

Most reputable driveway contractors list square-foot or linear-foot prices in the initial contract. Reference those same unit prices so nobody feels nickel-and-dimed.

Factor Idle Time

A Saturday change can mean crew overtime or a cancelled job the following Monday. Add 10–15 % for schedule disruption if the request is last-minute.

Document Everything with Photos

Before-and-after pictures attached to the change order eliminate “that crack was already there” debates.

Permits & Code Compliance

Many cities treat a widened driveway or added turn-around as a new impervious surface. If your change pushes total square footage past the allowable limit, you’ll need an updated permit.

Who Pulls the New Permit?

  • Usually the contractor, because they carry liability insurance.
  • Homeowner can pull it, but verify your lender doesn’t prohibit DIY permits.

Add Permit Cost to the Change Order

Include city fees, plan revision charges, and the contractor’s admin time ($75–$150 typical).

HOA Approval

If you live in a covenant community, attach HOA sign-off to the change order before work starts. A retroactive denial can force costly tear-outs.

Avoiding Disputes with Clear Documentation

1. Write Legibly or Type

Faded carbon copies kill your case in small-claims court.

2. Use Plain Language

“Install 1” × 12” expansion joint at garage apron per ASTM C920” is better than “fix crack by door.”

3. Set a Response Deadline

Give the homeowner 48 hours to sign or the price expires. It prevents sticker-shock arguments two weeks later.

4. Keep a Change Log

Staple all change orders to the original contract so you have one chronological file. Scan and back up to cloud storage.

Digital Tools That Speed Up Change Orders

  • CompanyCam: Snap photos, tag them to the change order, and email a PDF before you leave the site.
  • DocuSign: Legally binding e-signatures on phone; stores audit trail.
  • Jobber: Creates change orders from a quote in two clicks and syncs to QuickBooks.
  • Google Sheets offline mode: Edit in airplane-mode bad-coverage rural areas; auto-syncs when you hit Wi-Fi.

Quick Homeowner Checklist

  1. Read the original contract scope before asking for changes.
  2. Ask for the change order before the crew does the work.
  3. Verify price, schedule, and payment terms in writing.
  4. Check if the change affects permits or HOA rules.
  5. Keep digital copies in a shared Google Drive folder.
  6. Pay change-order invoices on time to keep lien rights off your property.
