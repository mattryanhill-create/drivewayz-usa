Driveway Lump Sum Contract: The Simplest Way to Budget Your Project

A Driveway Lump Sum Contract is a fixed-price agreement that locks in the total cost of your driveway installation or repair before the first shovel hits the ground. Instead of watching the meter run on hours and materials, you pay one predetermined amount—no surprises, no “extra” invoices, no sleepless nights.

At Drivewayz USA, roughly 80 % of residential jobs we complete are executed under this model because it gives homeowners three things they value most: certainty, speed, and control.

Why Homeowners Love a Driveway Lump Sum Contract

1. Budget Certainty

Knowing the final price up-front lets you plan the rest of your home-improvement calendar without fear of cost creep.

2. Faster Decision-Making

Contractors can order materials immediately after you sign, shortening lead times by 5–10 days on average.

3. Fewer Disputes

Because scope, specs, and price are fixed, disagreements over “extras” drop by almost 70 % compared with time-and-materials jobs, according to the National Association of Home Builders.

What’s Included in a Typical Driveway Lump Sum Contract?

Scope of Work

  • Removal and disposal of old pavement
  • Base preparation (grade, compact, geo-textile if needed)
  • Material type, thickness, and color
  • Edge restraints, joints, and drainage details
  • Sealer coat and curing period

Material Specifications

Insist on PSI strength for concrete (usually 4,000 PSI for driveways) or asphalt mix design (⅜-inch top coat, 19 mm base). These numbers should be spelled out line-by-line.

Project Schedule

A good contract lists start and completion dates, weather-related extensions, and daily work hours. Ask for a calendar-day count, not just “2–3 days” that can stretch into weeks.

Warranty Terms

Look for a minimum 2-year warranty on workmanship and 5-year on structural integrity. Cracks wider than ¼ inch within the first year should be patched at no charge.

Hidden Costs to Watch For

1. Soil Stabilization

Clay or organic soil may require lime or cement stabilization—$3–$5 extra per square foot.

2. Utility Crossings

If water or gas lines run beneath the driveway, you may need a sleeve or concrete encasement ($500–$1,200).

3. City Permits

Some municipalities treat a full driveway replacement as a “new cut” and charge $150–$400. Confirm who pulls the permit—you or the contractor.

4. Rain Delays

Most contracts allow 1–2 weather days at no cost; after that you could pay standby crew fees ($250/day). Negotiate a cap.

Step-by-Step: Signing a Driveway Lump Sum Contract

  1. Request Fixed-Price Bids Only

    Tell each contractor you will not accept T&M quotes. This keeps proposals apples-to-apples.

  2. Compare Line Items, Not Just Bottom Line

    One bid may skip rebar or sealer; another may include both. Adjust for missing items before you judge the “cheapest” offer.

  3. Verify Insurance & License

    Ask for certificates emailed directly from the carrier. A lump-sum price is meaningless if the contractor disappears mid-job.

  4. Negotiate Payment Milestones

    Typical: 10 % at signing, 40 % at material delivery, 40 % at substantial completion, 10 % final after 3-day walk-through.

  5. Initial the Drawings

    Even a simple sketch of layout, width, and drainage prevents “that’s not what I meant” debates later.

  6. Keep 10 % Retainage

    Hold back the last 10 % until you and the contractor sign a punch-list confirming every item is complete.

Red Flags That Void Fixed-Price Protection

Vague Allowances

Words like “allow $500 for aggregate” open the door to upsells. Demand exact quantities.

“Owner Supplied” Clauses

If you buy the sealer or reinforcement mesh yourself, the warranty may not cover failures related to those items.

Escalation Riders

Some asphalt contracts allow a price jump if oil futures rise >5 %. Strike this language or cap it at 3 %.

Hand-Written Changes

Always request a clean, revised contract PDF; scribbles in margins can be misread or “lost.”

DIY Pre-Contract Checklist (Print & Use)

  • ☐ Measure total square footage; photo existing cracks
  • ☐ Mark sprinkler heads and utility lines with flags
  • ☐ Decide on material color and finish (bring samples)
  • ☐ Check HOA rules on width, turn-around, color
  • ☐ Verify city setback and apron requirements
  • ☐ Ask neighbors for recent lump-sum invoices to benchmark price
  • ☐ Prepare site photos for contractors who can’t visit same day
