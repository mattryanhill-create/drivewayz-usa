A standard two-car driveway costs between $3,000 and $15,000 in 2026 — but the final price depends on the material you choose, your location, and several costs that most quotes don't mention upfront.

Cost by Material Type

The material you pick is the single biggest decision that shapes your final price. Here's how the most popular options compare, with real 2026 numbers.

Gravel — The Budget Option

Gravel is the cheapest driveway you can install. At $1–$3 per square foot, a 600 sq ft driveway costs as little as $600–$1,800. The average project runs about $1,800. It's a solid choice for long rural driveways or properties where a paved surface isn't required.

The tradeoff: gravel driveways only last 5–10 years before they need significant replenishment, and annual maintenance (raking, adding material) runs $100–$400 per year. If you want to understand how that adds up, check our guide on annual maintenance costs.

Asphalt — The Popular Middle Ground

Asphalt is one of the most common driveway materials in America. A new asphalt driveway costs $7–$13 per square foot installed, with an average total project cost of about $5,265 for a standard driveway. Replacement (which includes removing the old surface) runs $8–$15 per square foot.

If you already have asphalt in decent shape, an overlay costs just $3–$7 per square foot — a real money saver. Asphalt driveways last 15–30 years with proper sealing every 3–5 years. For a deeper dive, read our asphalt cost per square foot guide.

Concrete — Best Long-Term Value

A plain concrete driveway costs $5–$12 per square foot, while decorative options like stamped or colored concrete run $10–$20+ per square foot. The average concrete driveway project costs around $6,400, though it can range from $1,600 for a small single-car pad up to $32,000 for large decorative installations.

Concrete lasts 30–50 years — roughly double the lifespan of asphalt. That longer lifespan often makes concrete cheaper per year of use, even though the upfront price is higher. Labor accounts for about 50% of the total cost.

Pavers — Premium Look, Premium Price

Paver driveways average about $12,000 installed, with a range of $6,000–$24,000+ depending on the material. Concrete pavers run $10–$20 per square foot, brick pavers cost $20–$30, and natural stone can hit $20–$40 per square foot or more.

The upside is durability — brick pavers last 50–100 years, and damaged sections can be replaced individually instead of redoing the whole surface. For a complete look at long-term costs, see our replacement costs guide.

Resin-Bound — The Modern Choice

Resin-bound driveways are gaining popularity for their clean, modern look and permeable surface. They cost $11–$17 per square foot installed, putting a 600 sq ft driveway at $6,600–$10,200. Lifespan is around 25 years with minimal maintenance.

What Drives the Price Up

Your driveway quote isn't just about the paving material. Several factors can push your total well above the base price:

  • Grading and site prep: If your lot is uneven or sloped, grading costs $1,000–$5,125. Steep slopes add 20–40% to the total project cost.
  • Demolition of the old driveway: Removing existing concrete costs about $1,300 on average. Asphalt removal averages around $4,750, which includes hauling away the material.
  • Excavation: Digging out and prepping the ground adds $1,000–$2,500. If there are trees or bushes in the way, tree removal can run $1,500–$5,100.
  • Poor soil: Bad soil conditions (clay, peat, or sandy fill) can add 15–30% to your cost because extra base material and stabilization are needed.
  • Driveway shape: Curved or complex designs add 15–25% compared to a straight driveway. Limited access (narrow lots, tight streets) can add another 20–40%.
  • Reinforcement: Adding rebar or wire mesh to concrete costs $1–$3 per square foot extra — about $960–$1,280 on a standard driveway — but it prevents cracking and extends lifespan.
  • Your location: Costs in cities like Los Angeles, New York, and Boston run 30–50% above the national average. Rural areas tend to be 15–25% below average, though delivery costs can offset that savings.

Pro tip: Always ask your contractor whether the quote includes grading, base layer, and old driveway removal. Many base quotes don't, and those extras can add $2,000–$5,000 to the final bill.

Hidden Costs Most Homeowners Miss

These are the expenses that sneak up on homeowners after they've already committed to a project. Budget for them upfront so there are no surprises.

Permits and Inspections

Most municipalities require a permit for new driveway installations — typically $50–$200. If your driveway crosses public right-of-way (the area between your property line and the street), you may need a separate right-of-way permit. Inspection fees add another $50–$200. Some cities also require an engineering review for larger projects, which costs $200–$1,000.

The Driveway Apron

The apron — where your driveway meets the street — is often a separate line item. Apron installation averages $4,400, with a range of $1,300–$7,800. Some municipalities handle the curb cut themselves, but many don't. Check with your local public works department before you assume it's included.

Drainage

Contractors often discover drainage issues during site inspection. A French drain costs $1,000–$3,000, and a channel drain runs $500–$2,000. All driveways need to slope about 2% away from your house to prevent water pooling. If your lot doesn't naturally allow that, you'll need drainage work. Read more about drainage problems and how to fix them.

Sealing and Maintenance

Ongoing maintenance is a real cost that most people forget to factor into their budget:

  • Asphalt sealing (every 3–5 years): $1–$2 per sq ft, or $400–$800 for a typical driveway
  • Concrete sealing: $0.50–$1.50 per sq ft professionally, or $0.15–$0.35 per sq ft if you do it yourself
  • Gravel replenishment: $100–$400 per year

Edging and Curbing

Adding a clean edge to your driveway costs extra: concrete curbing runs $8–$15 per linear foot, Belgian block edging is $15–$30, and simple plastic edging costs $2–$5 per linear foot.

Heating Systems (Cold Climates)

Heated driveways eliminate the need for snow removal but add $3,000–$8,700 to the installation cost. Total cost including the heated system runs $12–$28 per square foot. Operating costs add $120–$600 per winter season.

2026 Price Trends

If you've been putting off your driveway project hoping prices would drop, here's the honest picture: driveway costs in 2026 are roughly 6–10% higher than 2023 prices, and multiple forces are pushing them up further.

The biggest factors driving 2026 driveway costs:

  • Material prices climbed 6.2% in 2025 — the largest annual increase since 2021's pandemic-era spike.
  • Tariffs on steel jumped prices 12.1%, which directly affects the rebar used in concrete driveways and the equipment contractors use.
  • Construction wages rose 4% annually, and construction employment actually fell slightly in 2025, meaning labor supply is tight and getting more expensive.
  • Asphalt paving mixtures increased 2.9% year-over-year, with Midwest pricing rising further due to oil costs and demand.
  • Proposed 25% tariffs on Canadian and Mexican cement could trigger regional concrete price spikes if fully implemented.

There is some good news: ready-mix concrete prices have softened slightly from their late-2024 peaks, and domestic supply has cushioned some of the tariff impact so far.

Bottom line: Driveway projects cost more than they did a few years ago, but prices have partially stabilized. The best strategy for 2026 is to lock in material prices early, get at least three contractor bids, and consider scheduling installation during the off-season (late fall or early spring) when contractors may offer better rates.

Does a New Driveway Increase Home Value?

Yes — and by more than you might expect. A new driveway typically returns 50–75% of its cost at resale, and a well-maintained driveway can increase your home's perceived value by up to 10%.

According to data tracked by Zillow, homes with paved driveways sell 7–10 days faster than those with gravel or dirt surfaces. And 72% of homebuyers say curb appeal influences their first impression of a property.

How to Save Money on Your Driveway

You don't have to cut corners to bring your driveway cost down. These strategies can save you hundreds or thousands:

  • Get at least three quotes. Contractor pricing varies widely — even in the same city, you'll see quotes that differ by 30–50%. Compare line by line, not just the total.
  • Schedule off-season. Late fall and early spring are slower periods for paving crews. Many contractors offer 10–15% discounts to keep their teams busy during the off-season.
  • Consider an overlay instead of full replacement. If your asphalt driveway is in fair condition, an overlay at $3–$7 per square foot costs roughly half what full replacement does.
  • Use recycled asphalt. Recycled or reclaimed asphalt runs $6.20–$8.75 per square foot — potentially saving up to $1,350 on a 600 sq ft driveway compared to virgin material.
  • Keep it simple. A straight driveway with a standard broom finish is always cheaper than curves, stamps, or decorative borders. The fancier the design, the higher the labor cost.
  • Do your own demo. If you're replacing an asphalt driveway, renting a jackhammer and hauling the debris yourself can save $1,000–$3,000 in demolition costs. (Concrete is heavier and harder to DIY.)
  • Bundle with neighbors. If a neighbor also needs driveway work, ask the contractor for a multi-job discount. Reduced mobilization costs give them room to negotiate.
  • Lock in material prices. With construction material prices still rising, signing a contract sooner rather than later can protect you from mid-project price increases.
